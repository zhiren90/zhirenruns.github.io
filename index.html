bio

My name is Zhiren. I am currently a geostructural engineer in the Detroit area. For my day job, I design underground shafts, earth retention systems, and building foundations ... and occasionally go for long walks inside sewers.

I received my undergraduate degree in civil engineering at University of Massachusetts, followed by graduate studies in solid mechanics at University of Minnesota and Johns Hopkins University. I left my PhD program in 2017 to pursue industry opportunities.

After enjoying two years of work-life balance, I regained a strong desire to probe the mechanical properties of materials in a rigorous manner. Luckily, I found the opportunity to work with Prof. Jon Estrada at the University of Michigan and have been serving as a visiting researcher at the Experimental Soft Mechanics Laboratory, while contemplating a formal return to academia.

My ongoing research utilizes continuum mechanics and viscoelasticity theories to characterize local, time-dependent features in soft polymeric and biological systems. I am generally fascinated by elegant experiments that are rooted in theories and capable of efficiently informing us of how materials and structures deform and fail. Particular soft spots include: optic-, acoustic-, and thermal-based processes to quantify stress and strain, scaling laws and size effects, and in vivo/vitro alternatives to traditional bulk property measurement procedures.

My side passion is the history of science and engineering. I am the creator of the Twitter series #dailymechanician, through which I hope to share bits and pieces of how the field has evolved, while putting faces to the names behind important equations and landmark papers.
