bio

My name is Zhiren. I am a Ph.D. student in the Solid Mechanics program at Brown University.

I conduct research in the Applied Mechanics Lab, under the guidance of Professor Haneesh Kesari. My ongoing project explores the mechanics behind traumatic brain injury, aiming to improve the efficiency and accuracy of tracking rigid body motion of skulls, assessing the applied dynamic loading in real-time, predicting the response of tissues and cells to forces and high-rate deformation, and inferring physiological properties from mechanical behaviors. With the principles of solid mechanics, I wish to lay the groundwork for improved disease and injury diagnostics, inspire minimally invasive medical procedures, and help save lives.

I am generally fascinated by elegant experiments that are rooted in theories and capable of efficiently informing us of how materials and structures deform and fail. Particular soft spots include: optic-, acoustic-, and thermal-based processes to quantify stress and strain, scaling laws and size effects, and in vivo/situ alternatives to traditional bulk property measurement procedures.

My side passion is the history of science and engineering. I am the creator of the Twitter series #DailyMechanician, through which I hope to share bits and pieces of how the field has evolved, while putting faces to the names behind important equations and landmark papers.

research

Real-time damage analysis for traumatic brain injuries (TBI)

This project is a part of the multi-university PANTHER program to understand, detect, and prevent TBI.

It is impractical to directly measure the deformation of a human brain during injury-inducing events. The overarching goal of our work is to indirectly yet accurately estimate the strains and strain-rates experienced by the brain tissues. Our strategy consists of the following elements: (1) a computational algorithm to reconstruct the rigid body motion of the skull via accelerometer data, (2) a wearable sensor system for evaluation of blast pressure, and (3) a theoretical framework to predict cell damage according to surface traction and displacement of the brain.

On a practical level, we aim to develop wearable products that can provide real-time injury risk analysis to atheletes, military personnel, and other high-risk populations. More fundamentally, we hope to inspire procedures to infer sub-surface medical conditions from perceivable surface phenomena, via the theories of mechanics.

Characterizing high-rate viscoelasticity via inertial cavitation

This work has been conducted under the mentorship of Prof. Jon Estrada at the University of Michigan since 2019.

Soft polymers and biological tissues are difficult to model theoretically, given their time-dependent and highly heterogeneous behaviors. It is even more challenging to experimentally characterize these materials. I have been improving the theoretical and computational framework behind IMR by: (1) introducing advanced viscoelastic laws to describe complex time-dependent behaviors, (2) implementing reduced-order modelling procedures to rapidly achieve inverse characterization.
